Capitalisation

Arria uses down-style capitalisation, meaning you should write words in lowercase unless there's a specific reason to capitalise them. For example, you capitalise proper nouns and the first word of each sentence.

Capitalising proper nouns

A proper noun is a specific name for an individual person, place, or thing. The opposite of a proper noun is a common noun, which refers to a general concept or thing. Here are some examples:

NOUN TYPE
country common
Canada proper
product common
Retine proper
Glower proper
Ointmo proper
sales common

The general rule is to capitalise proper nouns, no matter where they appear in the sentence. By contrast, you should capitalise common nouns (e.g. product) only when they appear at the start of the sentence. Therefore:

The top-performing country was Canada. Product sales in Canada increased by 24.2% overall. This was driven by improved sales of Retine (37.4%), Glower (14.3%), and Ointmo (7.2%).

However, bear in mind that common nouns can appear in the proper name for something. For example, sales can appear in the name of a specific team or department within the organization. In such cases, you should capitalise.

Product sales in Canada increased by 24.2%, owing to improved sales of Retine (37.4%), Glower (14.3%), and Ointmo (7.2%). We thank our colleagues in the Sales team for their efforts!

Capitalising trademarked names

Some trademarked names begin with a lowercase letter. Don't capitalise these, even if the name begins the sentence.

CORRECT iPad Pro sales are 5.6% below target.
INCORRECT IPad Pro sales are 5.6% below target.
CORRECT eBay is a multinational e-commerce company.
INCORRECT EBay is a multinational e-commerce company.

Capitalising headings and subheadings

For technical content, use sentence-case capitalisation for headings and subheadings.

In sentence case, you make most words in a heading or subheading lowercase. You capitalise the following:

  • the first word of the title or heading
  • the first word after a colon or em dash
  • abbreviations that use capital letters
  • proper nouns (the names of people, groups, or organizations)

Some example headings are provided below.

CORRECT Rule 1 — Capitalise proper nouns
INCORRECT Rule 1 — capitalise proper nouns
CORRECT Step 3: Connecting to an Arria Studio project
INCORRECT Step 3: connecting to a Arria Studio project
CORRECT REST APIs: How they work and what you need to know
INCORRECT REST APIs: how they work and what you need to know

Capitalising job titles

When you abbreviate a job title by using all caps — for example, CEO for Chief Executive Officer — use all caps no matter where the job title appears in the sentence. When writing a job title in full, follow the rules below.

Capitalise when the job title immediately precedes the person's name.

CORRECT Head of Technology (Development) Alasdair Logan joined Arria in 2010.
INCORRECT Head of technology (development) Alasdair Logan joined Arria in 2010.

Don't capitalise when the job title immediately follows the person's name.

CORRECT Alasdair Logan, head of technology (development), joined Arria in 2010.
INCORRECT Alasdair Logan, Head of Technology (Development), joined Arria in 2010.

Don't capitalise when the precedes the job title.

CORRECT Inform the chief security officer if you receive a phishing email.
INCORRECT Inform the Chief Security Officer if you receive a phishing email.

Don't capitalise when a possessive noun precedes the job title.

CORRECT Arria's vice president of marketing is Crystal Angel.
INCORRECT Arria's Vice President of Marketing is Crystal Angel.

Don't capitalise when a possessive adjective precedes the job title.

CORRECT Our vice president of marketing is Crystal Angel.
INCORRECT Our Vice President of Marketing is Crystal Angel.

Capitalising team names

The rule here is simple: the official name for a team is a proper noun, so you capitalise. The same applies for the names of specific groups, departments, committees, divisions, and so forth.

CORRECT Louise works in Marketing.
INCORRECT Louise works in marketing.
CORRECT Louise leads the Marketing team.
INCORRECT Louise leads the marketing team.

However, a word such as marketing can refer to a general area of expertise rather than the name of a specific team. In such cases, treat the word as a common noun and don't capitalise. Therefore, if Louise does marketing work for the Technical Content team:

CORRECT Louise does marketing work.
INCORRECT Louise does Marketing work.

CORRECT Louise is atrocious at marketing.
INCORRECT Louise is atrocious at Marketing.

Capitalising after a colon

Generally, don't capitalise the first word after a colon. You aren't starting a new sentence, so there's no need for it.

CORRECT The fix is simple: rename the column headers.
INCORRECT The fix is simple: Rename the column headers.

There are three exceptions:

  1. Capitalise the first word after a colon when it's a proper noun.
CORRECT Sales increased in two countries: France and Japan.
INCORRECT Sales increased in two countries: france and Japan.
  1. Capitalise the first word after a colon when writing a heading.
CORRECT REST APIs: How they work and what you need to know
INCORRECT REST APIs: how they work and what you need to know
  1. Capitalise when the colon introduces a direct quotation.
CORRECT Sharon Daniels, Arria CEO, is optimistic: "We are predicting big things for 2023."
INCORRECT Sharon Daniels, Arria CEO, is optimistic: "we are predicting big things for 2023."

Capitalising hyphenated words

Compound words are formed by merging two or more words into a single word.

They can be open (e.g. web page), closed (e.g. database), or hyphenated (e.g. add-in).

Most compounds are closed, but when a word in the compound is a proper noun or all-caps abbreviation, hyphens should be used. In these cases, the normal rules of capitalisation should also be followed.

CORRECT Remember the non-English-speaking people in your audience.
INCORRECT Remember the non-english-speaking people in your audience.
CORRECT Work in computer science frequently uses pre-Bayesian assumptions.
INCORRECT Work in computer science frequently uses pre-bayesian assumptions.
CORRECT Thirteen non-OPEC countries have signed an agreement to cut oil production.
INCORRECT Thirteen non-opec countries have signed an agreement to cut oil production.

Capitalising in quotations

Capitalise the first letter of a direct quotation when the quoted material is a complete sentence.

CORRECT Nietzsche wrote, "Without music, life would be a mistake."
INCORRECT Nietzsche wrote, "without music, life would be a mistake."

Don't capitalise when the quoted material is a fragment of the original complete sentence.

CORRECT Nietzsche wrote that life without music "would be a mistake."
INCORRECT Nietzsche wrote that life without music "Would be a mistake."

When writing a direct quotation with an interruption, don't capitalise the first word when you restart.

CORRECT "Without music," wrote Nietzsche, "life would be a mistake."
INCORRECT "Without music," wrote Nietzsche, "Life would be a mistake."

Capitalising references to other works

When referencing another work — for example, an article or book — follow whichever capitalisation style the publisher has used. Different publishers apply different conventions, and it's okay to reflect this diversity when citing works. For example:

Reiter and Dale explain this concept in Building Natural Language Generation Systems (2000). For a more detailed description and analysis of the SKILLSUM system, see Generating basic skills reports for low-skilled readers (2008) by Reiter and Williams.

Capitalising word–number combinations

A typical word–number combination is when you reference a specific appendix, image, page, section, or step in your documentation. For this, always capitalise the noun before the numeral.

CORRECT Refer to Appendix 2.
INCORRECT Refer to appendix 2.
CORRECT Read Page 17 of the manual.
INCORRECT Read page 17 of the manual.

The same principle applies to word–letter combinations.

CORRECT A comprehensive set of examples is included in Section A.
INCORRECT A comprehensive set of examples is included in section A.
