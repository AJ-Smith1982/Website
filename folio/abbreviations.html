Abbreviations

An abbreviation is the shortened form of a longer word or phrase. This topic covers five types of abbreviations: shortened words, contractions, initialisms, acronyms, and Latin abbreviations.

Abbreviations can improve your writing by making your sentences more concise and easier to read. Misused, they confuse your reader and make your writing appear illiterate or lazy. Follow the guidance below to ensure best practice.

Shortened words

Some shortened-word abbreviations omit all but the first few letters of a word, while others omit letters from the middle of the word. Note that abbreviations of this type often include a period at the end.

ABBREVIATION FULL
approx. approximately
Dr. Doctor
ed. edition
esp. especially
govt. government
Mr. Mister
p. page
Prof. Professor
s. section
v. volume
KEY

Don't use these unless the abbreviation represents a unit of measure or a title of address.

When abbreviating titles, remember to include a period at the end.

CORRECT Dr. Sripada plays a key role within our technology group.
INCORRECT Dr Sripada plays a key role within our technology group.

Don't use the abbreviated form unless the title holder's name directly follows.

CORRECT The professor is a leading authority on machine learning.
INCORRECT The Prof. is a leading authority on machine learning.

Note that some words have a long version and a short version. For example:

SHORT VERSION LONG VERSION
app application
demo demonstration
memo memorandum
photo photograph
sync synchronize

The words in the SHORT VERSION column are not abbreviations. Dr. is an abbreviation because it has the same pronunciation as Doctor. By contrast, app is just a short version of application and has its own pronunciation.

If the short version of a word is not an abbreviation, don't include a period at the end.

CORRECT Download our app from Microsoft AppSource.
INCORRECT Download our app. from Microsoft AppSource.
TIP

When you don't know if a term is an abbreviation or a shortened form of a word, pronounce it. Treat it as a word rather than an abbreviation if its pronunciation differs from the full version.

Contractions

Contractions use apostrophes to join words and show that letters are missing.

CONTRACTION FULL VERSION
aren't are not
can't cannot
don't do not
I'm I am
it's it is
we're we are
won't will not
you're you are

Unlike shortened-word abbreviations, contractions have a distinctive pronunciation. So, for example, Dr. is a shortened word because it's pronounced the same as Doctor, while you're is a contraction because it's pronounced differently to you are.

Contractions make your writing seem less formal and more conversational, which we generally see as positive. Therefore, we recommend that you use most types of contractions. For more details, refer to Abbreviations.

Initialisms and acronyms

Initialisms are abbreviations formed from the initial letters of a phrase. Here are some examples:

INITIALISM EXPANDED FORM PRONUNCIATION
AI artificial intelligence A, I
API application programming interface A, P, I
BI business intelligence B, I
CIA Central Intelligence Agency C, I, A
FBI Federal Bureau of Investigation F, B, I
NLG natural language generation N, L, G
NLP natural language processing N, L, P

Acronyms are similar to initialisms because they, too, are formed from the initial letters of other words. The difference is that you say acronyms as words, not by stating each initial. Here are some examples:

ACRONYM EXPANDED FORM PRONUNCIATION
GIF Graphics Interchange Format jif or gif *
JSON JavaScript Object Notation jay-son
NATO North Atlantic Treaty Organization nay-tow
POTUS President of the United States pow-tuhs

* The subject of much tedious argument. Merriam Webster helpfully gives both pronunciations!

Initialisms and acronyms are common in technical writing because they prevent the repetition of long, cumbersome phrases. Be careful, though. While concision is a hallmark of good writing, you should never achieve it at the expense of clarity. To avoid confusing your readers, it's good practice to define initialisms and acronyms at the point of first use.

CORRECT Cross-origin resource sharing (CORS) is a browser mechanism that enables a web application running under one domain to access resources in another domain.

When introducing an initialism or acronym, first spell out the full term and follow it with the abbreviation in parentheses, as shown above. If you reverse the order, enclose the complete term in parentheses.

ACCEPTABLE JSON (JavaScript Object Notation) is a lightweight data-interchange format.
TIP

You don't need to define every initialism or acronym; understand your audience and use discretion. No one reading your API specification needs reminding that API means application programming interface!

Latin abbreviations

Some Latin abbreviations frequently appear in English texts. The commonest are:

LATIN ABBREVIATION EXPANDED FORM PLAIN ENGLISH EQUIVALENT
AM ante meridiem before midday
e.g. exempli gratia for example
etc. et cetera and so forth
et al. et alia and other people
i.e. id est that is
PM post meridiem after midday
vs. versus against
KEY

Don't use a Latin abbreviation unless it appears in the table above.

We generally recommend avoiding Latin abbreviations, as they typically have straightforward English equivalents. If you choose to use one, ensure its correct usage by adhering to the rules given below.

Rule 1 — Don't use Latin abbreviations outside of parentheses

Generally, don't use a Latin abbreviation outside of parentheses.

CORRECT In English-speaking countries, the thousands separator is a comma and the decimal separator is a period. In other countries (e.g. Germany and Spain), the opposite is true.
INCORRECT In English-speaking countries, the thousands separator is a comma and the decimal separator is a period. In other countries — e.g. Germany and Spain — the opposite is true.

Outside parentheses, use the plain English equivalent instead.

CORRECT In English-speaking countries, the thousands separator is a comma and the decimal separator is a period. In other countries — for example, Germany and Spain — the opposite is true.

Make an exception for AM, PM, and vs. — these often appear outside parentheses.

>
CORRECT The next fixture is Chelsea vs. Real Madrid.

Rule 2 — Don't insert a comma after e.g. or i.e.

Some style guides advise inserting a comma directly after e.g. or i.e. We consider this excessive.

CORRECT This query groups the data for one measure (e.g. Sales) by one non-time dimension (e.g. Country) and calculates an aggregate value.
INCORRECT This query groups the data for one measure (e.g., Sales) by one non-time dimension (e.g., Country) and calculates an aggregate value.

Use a comma after the plain English equivalent.

CORRECT The values in a dimension field are known as dimension instances. For example, Canada and Germany are possible instances of the Country dimension.
INCORRECT The values in a dimension field are known as dimension instances. For example Canada and Germany are possible instances of the Country dimension.

Rule 3 — Use e.g. for examples and i.e. for specifics

Use e.g. to introduce examples.

CORRECT Don't use an alias (e.g. SalesDate) that starts with the name of another field (e.g. Sales).
INCORRECT Don't use an alias (i.e. SalesDate) that starts with the name of another field (i.e. Sales).

Use i.e. to introduce specifics.

CORRECT Arria Answers performs best when the size of the data file sent to the Arria Answers service (i.e. the data payload) is around 5MB or less.
INCORRECT Arria Answers performs best when the size of the data file sent to the Arria Answers service (e.g. the data payload) is around 5MB or less.

Rule 4 — Always use a period at the end of etc.

A common error is to forget the period at the end of etc.

CORRECT The editor uses colour mark-up to highlight the different ATL elements (variables, conditionals, etc.), making them easier to distinguish.
INCORRECT The editor uses colour mark-up to highlight the different ATL elements (variables, conditionals etc), making them easier to distinguish.

If etc. is the last word of a sentence, don't add a second period to terminate the sentence.

Rule 5 — Don't use etc. to indicate an incomplete list of people

To indicate an incomplete list of people, use et al. or its plain English equivalent.

CORRECT Chomsky's theory of universal grammar has many critics (see Sampson, Elman, and others), but it remains enormously influential.
INCORRECT Chomsky's theory of universal grammar has many critics (see Sampson, Elman, etc.), but it remains enormously influential.

Given that et al. is mostly used in academic contexts, we strongly recommend using its plain English equivalent instead. Reserve the abbreviation for situations where space is limited, such as in footnotes.
